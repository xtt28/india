4500 years ago, a civilization emerged.

The Indus River Valley civilization in the land of what is now India, characterized by thriving trade routes and groundbreaking religious developments, was the driver of the cultural landscape and economy that would distinguish the Indian subcontinent to this day.

Northern Indian Kingdoms

550

Fall of the Gupta Empire

After the fall of the Gupta Empire, the northern and southern sides of India had different political systems. Disunity was rampant, with constant power disputes occurring in the north and military enemies leveraging this.

But this division also had a good side: it led to the development of diverse cultures and literature. India had already inherited scriptures, language, a pantheon of gods, rituals, and a social class system from the Aryans, people who migrated to India around 2000 BC. This culture inherited and advanced by the Indians would serve as the foundation for its future impact on its region.

606–648

Harsha's Kingdom

Ruins of Nalanda, Bihar. https://www.worldhistory.org/Harsha/

Temporary unity was restored to northern India with the reign of Harsha, a leader known for his "piety, liberality, and even scholarship." While being attentive to political manners, he also cared about his subjects, supporting hospitals and regularly giving them great gifts.

Although he was a Buddhist, Harsha was acceptive of the faiths around him. Due to a decline in trade and commerce, guilds of traders and merchants were dissolved, so the economies of his kingdom were increasingly feudal and self-sufficient.

King Harsha temporarily unified northern India thanks to his ability to hold relationships with the small states of the fragmented kingdom. Despite this, the kingdom fell rapidly upon his assassination, undoing the progress in unification that Harsha made.

1206–1526

Sultanate of Delhi

Intending to harvest the wealth from its temples, Mahmud of Ghazni, a Turkic leader, "annexed several states in northwestern India and the Punjab" between 1001 and 1027, partially contributing to Buddhism's decline in India. Despite their destruction of Buddhist and Hindu sites and creation of Islamic structures, not many Indians converted to Islam.

That changed in 1206, when Mahmud's successors established the sultanate of Delhi—an Islamic state in northern India. It peaked in the 14th century, being among the most powerful Islamic nations, while having limited authority outside of its center of power in Delhi. Islamic government still failed to gain prominence within India, with the sultans of Delhi exerting unstable and weak control over their subjects.

The economy was largely centered on agricultural production; in addition, copper and silver coins were used as currency. In terms of religion, "the sultans prominently sponsored Islam and helped to establish a secure place for their faith in the cultural landscape of India."

Southern Indian Kingdoms

850–1267

Kingdom of Chola

The kingdom of Chola emerged in the politically fragmented region of southern India. Local regions had a considerable amount of autonomy, with the kingdom having a mainly decentralized political system. During the kingdom's high point in the 11th century, its forces conquered Ceylon and some of southeast Asia.

Hinduism was the main religion of the Cholas, although Buddhism and Jainism also gained some traction. The economy was strengthened by trade, contributing to elements such as a strong military. Despite this, the kingdom fell due to "native Sinhalese forces [expelling] Chola officials from Ceylon and revolts [erupting] in southern India."

1336–1565

Kingdom of Vijayanagar

Ruins of a Vijayanagar temple. https://smarthistory.org/art-and-architecture-of-vijayanagara-empire/

The Kingdom of Vijayanagar, originating in 1336, was the second kingdom to control most of southern India. It was established by two brothers, Harisha and Bukka, dispatched from the Sultanate of Delhi to expand the kingdom to the south. Unlike the kingdom's intentions, the two brothers thought of this exploration as a chance to become powerful rulers of a new kingdom.

To increase their popularity, the rulers stopped practicing Islam, returning to Hindu beliefs and earning the kingdom its name: Vijayanagar, meaning "city of victory." Despite this change and the kingdom's young age, they maintained excellent trade relationships with the Muslims, continuing their half-a-millennium bond.

The Kingdom of Vijayanagar eventually fell to Mughal conquerors in 1565.
